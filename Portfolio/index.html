Undergraduate Honours Research • Propulsion Development

NERVA-ONE Rocket Engine

NERVA-ONE is a 1 kilonewton regeneratively cooled ethanol-70 / nitrous oxide liquid rocket engine developed as part of undergraduate honours research at Macquarie University. The project focuses on the integration of thermal-fluid analysis, regenerative cooling optimisation, additive manufacturing constraints, and propulsion systems engineering into a single manufacturable engine architecture.

A major focus of the project was the development of a custom one-dimensional optimisation framework capable of generating continuously varying regenerative cooling channel geometry and variable hot-side wall thickness based on local thermal and structural loading conditions.

Rather than acting as a purely theoretical design study, NERVA-ONE was developed around physical manufacturability and future validation testing. The engine is intended to demonstrate how advanced design tools and additive manufacturing can make small-scale liquid propulsion research more accessible within a university environment.

Injector, Manifold & Cooling System

The injector uses a triplet unlike-impinging configuration to promote atomisation of ethanol and nitrous oxide prior to combustion. Small orifice sizes required the injector to be treated as a precision manufacturing challenge as much as a propulsion design problem.

The manifold and thrust chamber were developed around additive manufacturing constraints, enabling complex internal flow routing and cooling features that would be difficult or impractical to manufacture using conventional machining.

Thermal management is achieved through a combination of regenerative cooling and film cooling. Regenerative channels remove heat through the chamber wall, while film cooling reduces local wall heat flux near the combustion region.

Novelty & Design Approach

Variable Wall Thickness

The thrust chamber uses variable hot-side wall thickness along its length, allowing the structure to respond to local pressure, thermal loading, and material limitations instead of relying on a constant wall assumption.

Optimised Regen Channels

Regenerative cooling channel geometry is generated through a custom optimisation workflow, allowing local channel dimensions and flow behaviour to be adjusted along the chamber and nozzle.

Low-Part Engine Architecture

The engine is designed around three primary components: thrust chamber, manifold, and injector. This reduces assembly complexity and supports a clearer validation pathway.

Computational Optimisation Workflow

A custom Python-based optimisation workflow was developed to support the regenerative cooling design. The tool discretises the thrust chamber along its axial length and evaluates local thermal, pressure, and structural requirements to guide the cooling channel geometry.

This approach allows channel height, width, helix angle, wall thickness, pressure drop, and coolant temperature rise to be considered together. The result is a more integrated design process where thermal performance, structural reliability, and manufacturability are solved as a coupled problem.

Future Development

Static Hot-Fire Validation

The next major milestone is experimental validation through static hot-fire testing to compare measured engine behaviour against theoretical and computational predictions.

Expanded Optimisation Tool

Further development will focus on a user-facing version of the cooling tool, allowing future users to modify engine parameters without directly editing the codebase.

Advanced Cooling Geometries

Future work will compare bifurcated and non-bifurcated cooling channel strategies to better understand pressure drop, manufacturability, and thermal performance trade-offs.

Monolithic SLM Architecture

Long-term development aims to move toward more integrated, single-part SLM-manufactured engine architectures with reduced assembly complexity.
